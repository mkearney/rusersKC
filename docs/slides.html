"Replication crisis"

  • Recent discovery that most attempts to reproduce previous scientific research fail.
  • Originated in psychology, but applies to all social science.
  • There are a lot of potential causes, but people like to agree on 2 in particular...

1. p-hacking

  • p-hacking refers to systematic biases favoring significant (e.g., p < .05) results in analysis and peer review stages of research
  • Result of p-hacking is literature with:
    • many reported false positives
    • few reported null [debunking] findings

 

2. Open data

  • Replication crisis says more about documenting the scientific process than the validity of previous findings
  • Leading cause of replication failure has been poor documentation
    • unpublished data
    • missing or misnamed variables
    • ommitted procedures

Big data

  • Big data refers to "datasets that are too large for traditional data-processing systems and that therefore require new echnologies" (Provost & Fawcett, 2013, p. 54)
  • "Big data" isn't really that different from "small data"
    • It has rows, more columns, or more rows and more columns

Dealing with big data

  • The phrase "big data" is useful as a heuristic for new digital sources of data
  • New sources of data tend to be plentiful and less structured
  • To deal with new digital sources of data, scholars have begun turning to data science

Data science

  • Data science is "a set of fundamental principles that support and guide the principled extraction of information and knowledge from data" (Provost & Fawcett, 2013, p. 52)
  • More practically, data science combines both quantitative statistics and computer science methods

Communication researchers should leverage data science tools and methods.

Better samples

  • Convenience samples of students tend to be homogenous and limited in size
  • Digital sources are not restricted by geography and can be massive
  • In a few hours, one could, for example, stream a million tweets from the U.S.

 

Better samples

  • Digital [online] sources of data can be gathered across space and time
  • Scholars often criticize these samples because they "are not representative of the general population"
    • But people who live in glass houses shouldn't throw stones
  • Internet activity correlates strongly with people activity

 

Bigger questions

  • Big data and data science make it possible to conduct research on a larger and more dynamic scale
  • For example, one could collect tweets about political candidates daily and analyze change in sentiment over time

 

Research questions

  • Measurement error is not always a problem, but sometimes it is
  • Data science makes it possible to examine phonomena without measurement error
  • For example, people tend to overestimate the degree to which their social media networks are politically heterogeneous

 

User networks

  • In my dissertation, I tracked user networks on Twitter during the 2016 election
  • Republican group: users who followed one or more of the following:
    • Sean Hannity, Sarah Palin, Fox News Politics, and Drudge Report
  • Democrat group: users who followed one or more of the following:
    • Rachel Maddow, Paul Krugman, HuffPost Politics, and Salon

 

 

Network analysis

  • Network analysis makes it possible to more accurately create a visual depiction of network clusters
  • The degree to which Twitter networks are organized by partisanship is especially striking when examined via network analysis

 

 

 

 

Communication researchers have an obligation to use data science.

Big data baggage

  • In addition to computational demands, big data creates new ethical concerns for researchers
  • Data science is becoming the intersection of prediction-centered machine learning and theory-centered statistics (Vasant, 2013)
  • To better understand these two approaches, it helps to compare the goals of statistics with the goals of computer science

Two approaches

Computer science examines whether a certain pattern can be found in data.

  • e.g., hey Alexa, what is the capital of Chile?

Statistics examines whether data can be found in a certain pattern.

  • e.g., lack of sleep correlates with decreased cognitive performance

Two approaches

  • Statistics, more than computer science, is condusive to knowledge discovery (Vasant, 2013)
    • Computer science provides tools for summarizing and subsetting "databases"
    • Statistics provides tools for testing and creating theories with "data frames"

Competing models

  • Quantitative statistics models are judged by their explanatory potential
  • Machine learning models are statistical models judged solely by accuracy of their predictions
    • If we had 100 cases of data, then we would fit a model on 50 of those cases and test the model on the other 50 cases
  • Predictive models do not explain what is happening
    • Impossible to know whether variables in model actually reflect some underlying construct (Vasant, 2013)

Machine learning problems

  • Machine learning is agnostic toward theoretical considerations, which leads to at least 2 problems
    • Predictive models can be misinterpreted as explanatory, e.g., racial profiling
    • Algorithms can be manipulated (Verge, "Twitter taught Microsoft’s AI chatbot to be a racist asshole in less than a day", 2016)

Theory hangs in the balance

  • Institutions will increasingly make decisions informed by data science
  • The future of data science will be shaped by machine learning and statistics
  • Communication researchers are uniquely suited to contribute to best practices of data science

Communication studies

  • Communication studies is a natural fit for data science
  • Digital communication means "messages" can be directly captured using computational methods
    • So the "data" in data science is communication
  • Methodological diversity in communication studies can provide insights to both applied and theoretical understandings of big data and data science
